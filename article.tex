\Abstract{
Indirect treatment comparisons (ITCs) are essential in Health Technology Assessment (HTA) when head-to-head clinical trials are absent. A common challenge arises when attempting to compare a treatment with available individual patient data (IPD) against a competitor with only reported aggregate-level data (ALD), particularly when trial populations differ in effect modifiers. By streamlining the workflow of covariate simulation, model standardization, and contrast estimation, \pkg{outstandR} enables robust and compatible evidence synthesis in complex decision-making scenarios.

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}

Indirect treatment comparisons (ITCs) are essential in Health Technology Assessment (HTA) when head-to-head clinical trials are absent. A common and significant challenge arises when attempting to compare a treatment with available individual patient data (IPD) against a competitor with only reported aggregate-level data (ALD). When trial populations differ in effect modifiers, indirect comparisons that do not adjust for covariates are biased; thus, population adjustment methods are increasingly used to bridge these cross-trial differences \citep{Phillippo2016DSU}.


Well-established methods for this scenario include Matching-Adjusted Indirect Comparison (MAIC)  \citep{signorovitch2010} and Simulated Treatment Comparison (STC) \citep{caro2010,ishak2015}. However, these methods often face methodological limitations, such as sensitivity to poor covariate overlap (in MAIC) or the inability to extrapolate beyond observed covariate spaces. Furthermore, from a practical standpoint, software implementations for these methods have often been fragmented, limited in scope, or reliant on ad-hoc scripts.


To address methodological limitations, the literature has moved toward advanced approaches like G-computation \citep{RemiroAzocar2022a}, Multiple Imputation Marginalization (MIM) \citep{RemiroAzocar2022b} and Multilevel Network Meta-Regression (ML-NMR) \citep{phillippo_multinma_2020}. G-computation and MIM allows for model-based standardization via covariate simulation, overcoming limited patient-level data. There has been active debate regarding whether population-average conditional or marginal estimands are most suitable for decision-making \citep{remiro2025marginal}. While some argue for conditional estimands to increase power \citep{remiro2021conflating}, others advocate for marginal estimands \citep{phillippo2021target}. Recent syntheses and harmonisation suggest that both are valuable depending on the decision question \citep{phillippo2025effect}. Consequently, it is beneficial for analysts to have the capability to compare multiple approaches within a single analysis.

This article introduces \pkg{outstandR}, an \proglang{R} package designed to address these inefficiencies by providing a comprehensive and unified framework for population-adjusted indirect comparison (PAIC). \pkg{outstandR} streamlines the entire workflow—from covariate simulation and model standardization to contrast estimation. Beyond standard weighting (MAIC) and regression (STC) approaches, the package implements advanced G-computation and MIM methods within both maximum likelihood and Bayesian frameworks.

\section{Population-Adjusted Indirect Comparison (PAIC)}
\label{sec:methodology}
Consider a scenario with two trials: an $AC$ trial with individual patient data (IPD) comparing treatments $A$ and $C$, and a $BC$ trial with only published aggregate-level data (ALD) comparing treatments $B$ and $C$. The objective is to estimate a comparison of the effects of treatments $A$ and $B$ on a suitable scale in some target population $P$, denoted by the parameter $d_{AB(P)}$ \citep{RemiroAzocar2022a}. We will focus on population adjustment methods to make ``anchored'' indirect comparisons, where the evidence is connected by a common comparator, in our case $C$, and ``unanchored'' indirect comparisons, where the evidence is disconnected due to the lack of a common comparator or single-arm studies.

\subsection{General problem formulation}
\label{ssec:problem_formulation}

Let $\mu_{A(AC)}$ and $\mu_{C(AC)}$ represent the expected outcomes for treatments $A$ and $C$ within the $AC$ trial population. We also have estimators $\bar{Y}_{A(AC)}$ and $\bar{Y}_{C(AC)}$ of the expected outcomes. There is also an equivalent set of parameters for the $B$ trial. 

We define estimators $\Delta_{AC(AC)}$ and $\Delta_{BC(BC)}$ of the relative treatment effects at the trial level for a given scale. Assuming a {\it difference} scale (e.g., log-odds ratio, risk difference), the estimate of relative treatment effect between treatments $A$ and $C$
\begin{equation}
\label{eqn:relative_treatment_effect}
\Delta_{AC(AC)} = g(\bar{Y}_{C{(AC)}}) - g(\bar{Y}_{A{(AC)}}),
\end{equation}
and similarly for $\Delta_{BC(BC)}$. If we assume that there is no difference in effect modifiers between trials, then the estimator of the relative treatment effect $d_{AB(BC)}$ is
\[
\Delta_{AB} = \Delta_{BC(BC)} - \Delta_{AC(AC)}.
\]
However, when distributions of the effect modifiers are different between trial populations, the relative treatment effect estimated from each trial cannot simply be combined as above. This is the purpose of population-adjustment in ITC, to enable the comparison of unbiased statistics
\begin{equation}
\label{eqn:paic}
\Delta_{AB(BC)} = \Delta_{BC(BC)} - \Delta_{AC(BC)}.
\end{equation}
Section~\ref{sec:paic-methods} will detail the PAIC methods available in \pkg{outstandR}.

\subsection{Population adjustment methods}
\label{sec:paic-methods}
The \pkg{outstandR} package allows the implementation of a range of methods for adjusting the IPD to the target population, commonly the ALD.

We make a clear and necessary distinction between prognostic factors (PF) and effect modifiers (EM). Prognostic factors are covariates that affect the outcome whereas effect modifiers are covariates that alter the effect of treatment as measured on a given scale. Effect modifiers are not necessarily also prognostic variables, and may be specific to each treatment. Note that effect modifier status on one scale does not necessarily imply effect modifier status on another scale. We also assume internal validity of the studies included in the analysis. That is, the studies provide unbiased estimates of treatment effects in their respective sample populations.

\subsubsection{Matching-Adjusted Indirect Comparison (MAIC)}
Matching-Adjusted Indirect Comparison (MAIC) is based on propensity score weighting, reweights IPD to match aggregate characteristics of the comparator trial \citep{signorovitch2010}. The core idea of MAIC is to re-weight the patients in the IPD $AC$ trial such that their aggregated baseline characteristics match those of the ALD $BC$ trial. This re-weighting process is essentially an optimization problem.

Let $X_i$ be the vector of baseline characteristics for the $i$-th patient in the IPD $AC$ trial, for $i = 1, \dots, n_A$, where $n_A$ is the number of patients in $AC$ trial. Let $\bar{X}_B$ be the vector of mean baseline characteristics reported for the ALD $BC$ trial.

The objective is to find a set of weights, $w_i$, for each patient $i$ in the $AC$ trial, such that the weighted average of their baseline characteristics matches the aggregate baseline characteristics of the $BC$ trial. This can be formulated as minimizing a certain objective function, typically related to the sum of weights or the effective sample size, subject to matching constraints.

A common approach involves a logistic regression-based re-weighting. The weights $w_i$ are often derived from a propensity score-like model, where the parameters of this model are optimized. Specifically, the weights are defined as:

$$ 
w_i = \exp(\beta^\top X_i),
$$
where $\beta$ is a vector of coefficients to be estimated. The optimization problem then aims to find $\beta$ such that the weighted means of the covariates in the IPD dataset match the target means from the ALD dataset. This is equivalent to solving the following set of moment-matching equations:

$$ 
\sum_{i=1}^{n_A} w_i X_i = \sum_{i=1}^{n_A} \exp(\beta^\top X_i) X_i = n_A \bar{X}_B.
$$
This system of equations can be rewritten by centering the covariates around the target means $\bar{X}_B$:

$$ 
\sum_{i=1}^{n_A} \exp(\beta^\top (X_i - \bar{X}_B)) (X_i - \bar{X}_B) = 0. 
$$
This is the first derivative with respect to $\beta$ of the following convex objective function, which is typically minimized:

$$
\min_{\beta \in \mathbb{R}^p} \sum_{i=1}^{n_A} \exp(\beta^\top (X_i - \bar{X}_B)),
$$
where $p$ is the number of baseline characteristics being matched. The minimization of this convex function ensures a unique global minimum for $\beta$. Once $\beta$ is found, the weights $w_i$ are calculated, and these weights are then used in a weighted analysis (e.g., weighted logistic regression or Cox regression) of the IPD from the $AC$ trial to estimate the treatment effect in a population that is adjusted to resemble the $BC$ trial.

The resulting effect size from the weighted IPD in the $AC$ trial can then be indirectly compared to the treatment effect observed in the $AB$ trial (often compared to its common comparator in an ``anchored'' MAIC, or as a direct comparison in ``unanchored'' MAIC). The optimization process ensures that the populations being compared are as similar as possible based on the available covariates, thereby reducing potential bias due to differences in prognostic factors or effect modifiers.

\subsubsection{Simulated Treatment Comparison (STC)}
Simulated Treatment Comparison (STC) relies on outcome regression models fitted to IPD, conditioning on covariates, then applying estimates to ALD \citep{caro2010, ishak2015}. STC involves using the IPD to build a model that predicts patient outcomes based on their baseline characteristics, and then using this model to estimate what the outcomes of the IPD trial's treatment would have been if it were applied to the population of the ALD $BC$ trial.

\paragraph{Model Fitting (Optimization Step)}
Let $Y_i$ be the outcome for patient $i$ in the IPD $AC$ trial, and $X_i$ be the vector of baseline characteristics for patient $i$. An outcome regression model is fitted using the IPD $AC$ trial. This model relates the outcome to the baseline covariates. For a continuous outcome, a linear regression might be used; for a binary outcome, logistic regression; and for time-to-event data, a Cox proportional hazards model or parametric survival model.
The general form of this outcome model can be expressed as:
$$ 
g(\mathbb{E}[Y_i | X_i]) = \alpha + \beta^\top X_i,
$$
where $g(\cdot)$ is a link function (e.g., identity for linear regression, logit for logistic regression, log for Cox regression hazard ratio); $\mathbb{E}[Y_i | X_i]$ is the expected outcome for patient $i$ given their baseline characteristics $X_i$; $\alpha$ is the intercept; $\beta$ is a vector of regression coefficients for the baseline characteristics $X_i$.
The ``optimization'' in this step refers to the standard process of estimating the model parameters ($\alpha$ and $\beta$) by maximizing the likelihood function (or minimizing a sum of squares, depending on the model).

\paragraph{Prediction (Simulation Step)}
Once the outcome model is fitted using data from the IPD $AC$ trial, the estimated coefficients ($\hat{\alpha}, \hat{\beta}$) are used to predict the expected outcome of treatment $A$ for the population of the $AB$ trial. Since only aggregate data (typically means and standard deviations) are available for the $AB$ trial's population, these aggregate values ($\bar{X}_B$) are substituted into the fitted model to estimate the mean outcome.

The predicted mean outcome of treatment $A$ in the population of the $BC$ trial, denoted as $\hat{\mathbb{E}}[Y_A | \text{Pop}_B]$, is calculated as:
$$
\hat{\mathbb{E}}[Y_A | \text{Pop}_B] = g^{-1}(\hat{\alpha} + \hat{\beta}^\top \bar{X}_B),
$$
where $g^{-1}(\cdot)$ is the inverse of the link function.

\paragraph{Limitations and modifications}
A significant limitation of standard STC arises when the link function $g(\cdot)$ is non-linear (e.g., logit or log link). In such cases, simply substituting the mean covariate values ($\bar{X}_B$) into the fitted model can lead to biased estimates of the marginal treatment effect in the target population (known as ``aggregation bias'' or ``non-collapsibility'').

To address this, more advanced STC-type methods, particularly those incorporating actual simulation, are being developed. These methods aim to correctly estimate the marginal outcome in the target population, often by simulating individual patient profiles for the ALD trial's population based on their reported aggregate statistics and assumed covariate distributions. This approach is truly simulation-based and involves drawing from estimated joint covariate distributions and leads into the following methods based on G-computation.

\subsubsection{Parametric G-computation with Maximum Likelihood}
G-computation, also known as the G-formula, fits an outcome model to IPD using maximum likelihood, then predicts outcomes in the comparator population \citep{RemiroAzocar2022a}. It is a method for estimating causal effects by standardizing outcomes across different treatment regimens. The conditional expectation of the outcome given treatment and covariates are modelled directly. When implemented using Maximum Likelihood Estimation (MLE), the process involves fitting a series of regression models for the outcome and, potentially, for time-varying confounders, and then using these models to predict outcomes under hypothetical treatment scenarios.

The fundamental idea of G-computation is to emulate a randomized trial by predicting outcomes under different treatment assignments for each individual in the observed (or a representative) population, and then averaging these predicted outcomes.
The target parameter for G-computation is often the marginal mean outcome under a specific treatment regimen $A^*$, defined as:
$$
\mathbb{E}[Y^{A^*}] = \sum_{x} \mathbb{E}[Y | A^*=a, X=x] P(X=x).
$$
For continuous covariates, the sum becomes an integral. In essence, it calculates the average outcome if all members of the population followed the treatment regimen $A^*$.

\paragraph{Maximum Likelihood Implementation Steps}
The MLE implementation of G-computation typically involves the following steps:

First, specify and fit models for outcome and confounders. This is the ``optimization'' step where parameters are estimated using MLE. A set of regression models are specified for the outcome and any time-varying confounders.

\begin{enumerate}
    \item Outcome Model: A model for the outcome $Y$ given the treatment $A$ (or sequence of treatments $A_t$) and relevant covariates $L$ (baseline and/or time-varying confounders).
    $$
    \mathbb{E}[Y | A, L] = h(\theta_Y^\top Z_Y),
    $$
    where $h(\cdot)$ is the inverse of the link function, $Z_Y$ is a vector of covariates and treatment terms, and $\theta_Y$ are the parameters to be estimated. For example, for a continuous outcome $Y$:
    $$
    Y \sim N(\beta_0 + \beta_A A + \beta_L L + \dots, \sigma^2).
    $$
    The parameters $(\beta_0, \beta_A, \beta_L, \dots, \sigma^2)$ are estimated by maximizing the likelihood of the observed data.
    \item Confounder Models (for time-varying confounders): If there are time-varying confounders $L_t$ that are themselves affected by prior treatment and affect future outcomes, separate models are fitted for each $L_t$ given prior covariates and treatment history.
    $$
    \mathbb{E}[L_t | A_{0 \dots t-1}, L_{0 \dots t-1}] = f(\theta_{L_t}^\top Z_{L_t}),
    $$
    where $f(\cdot)$ is a link function, $Z_{L_t}$ includes relevant past treatment and covariate history, and $\theta_{L_t}$ are the parameters estimated by maximizing the likelihood for each confounder model.
\end{enumerate}
The estimation of these parameters ($\theta_Y, \theta_{L_t}$) constitutes the maximum likelihood optimization. For each model, we seek the parameters that maximize the likelihood function $\mathcal{L}(\theta | \text{Data})$, or equivalently, minimize the negative log-likelihood.

\paragraph{Simulation/Prediction Step}
Once all necessary models are fitted, the estimated parameters are used to predict outcomes under a hypothesized treatment. This can be done via:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item Monte Carlo Simulation (typically for complex scenarios):
     For each individual in the original dataset (or a large simulated population representative of it):
     Set their treatment to the hypothesized regimen $A^*$. Use the fitted confounder models to simulate trajectories of time-varying confounders under this regimen. Use the fitted outcome model to predict the outcome for each individual under the simulated covariate and treatment path. Average the predicted outcomes across all individuals to obtain the marginal mean outcome for the treatment $A^*$.

    \item Direct Standardization (for simpler cases without time-varying confounding):
     For each observed individual $i$, predict their outcome under the hypothesized treatment $A^*$:
     $$
     \hat{Y}_i(A^*) = h(\hat{\theta}_Y^\top Z_Y(A^*, L_i)),
     $$
     where $Z_Y(A^*, L_i)$ incorporates the hypothesized treatment $A^*$ for individual $i$ with their observed covariates $L_i$.
     The estimated marginal mean outcome is then the average of these predictions:
     $$
     \hat{\mathbb{E}}[Y^{A^*}] = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^N \hat{Y}_i(A^*).
     $$
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{Synthetic cohort generation}
Generally, there are several different approaches to generating a synthetic cohort. We will detail how to create one which accurately reflects the target population's covariate structure by employing a {\it Gaussian copula}. This allows us to decouple the dependence structure (correlation) from the marginal distributions of the individual covariates.

Let $K$ be the number of covariates and $\boldsymbol{\Sigma}$ be the $K \times K$ correlation matrix, typically estimated from the IPD. We wish to simulate $N$ individuals for the pseudo-population.

The simulation proceeds in three steps:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Generate Latent Multivariate Normal Data:}
    We first draw $N$ samples from a multivariate normal distribution with mean vector $\mathbf{0}$ and correlation matrix $\boldsymbol{\Sigma}$:
    \begin{equation*}
        \mathbf{Z}_i \sim MVN_K(\mathbf{0}, \boldsymbol{\Sigma}), \quad \text{for } i = 1, \dots, N.
    \end{equation*}

    \item \textbf{Probability Integral Transform:}
    We transform these correlated normal variates into uniform variates $\mathbf{U}_i = (U_{i1}, \dots, U_{iK})$ using the cumulative distribution function (CDF) of the standard normal distribution, $\Phi(\cdot)$:
    \begin{equation*}
        U_{ik} = \Phi(Z_{ik}),
    \end{equation*}
    where $U_{ik} \sim \text{Uniform}(0, 1)$ and the correlation structure of $\mathbf{Z}$ is preserved.

    \item \textbf{Inverse Probability Transform (Marginals):}
    Finally, we map the uniform variates to the target scales using the inverse CDFs (quantile functions) of the marginal distributions. Let $F_k(\cdot; \theta_k)$ denote the CDF for the $k$-th covariate with parameters $\theta_k$:
    \begin{equation}
        \label{eqn:marginal-transformation}
        X_{ik} = F_k^{-1}(U_{ik}; \theta_k).
    \end{equation}
\end{enumerate}

This procedure ensures that the resulting synthetic covariates $\mathbf{X}$ possess the desired marginal properties defined by $F_k$ while maintaining the correlation structure $\boldsymbol{\Sigma}$.

\subsubsection{Parametric G-computation with Bayesian Inference}
G-computation (G-formula) can also be implemented using Bayesian inference. This is similar to the maximum likelihood version, but within a Bayesian framework allows for coherent propagation of uncertainty \citep{RemiroAzocar2022a}.
This offers several advantages over the frequentist MLE approach, particularly in quantifying uncertainty and incorporating prior knowledge. Instead of finding point estimates for model parameters that maximize a likelihood function, the Bayesian approach involves estimating the full posterior distribution of these parameters. This allows for direct probability statements about the parameters and the resulting causal effects.

As before, we wish to estimate the marginal mean outcome under a specific treatment regimen $A^*$, defined as $\mathbb{E}[Y^{A^*}]$. The core idea is to average predicted outcomes under counterfactual treatment scenarios.
The Bayesian implementation of G-computation typically follows these steps:

\paragraph{Step 1: Specify and Fit Models with Bayesian Inference}

\begin{enumerate}
    \item Model Specification: Define the same types of regression models as in the frequentist approach for the outcome
        $$
        Y_i \sim g^{-1}(\alpha + \beta^\top X_i),
        $$
        where $g^{-1}(\cdot)$ is the inverse of the link function, $X_i$ includes treatment and covariate terms, and $\alpha, \beta$ are the parameters.

    \item Prior Specification: Assign prior distributions to all model parameters (e.g., $\alpha, \beta, \gamma$, and any dispersion parameters like $\sigma^2$). These priors reflect existing knowledge or assumptions about the parameters before observing the data. For example:
    $$ \alpha \sim N(0, \sigma_{\alpha}^2) $$
    $$ \beta_k \sim N(0, \sigma_{\beta}^2) \quad \forall k $$
    $$ \sigma^2 \sim \text{Inv-Gamma}(a, b). $$

    \item Posterior Sampling (Optimization/Inference):
    Using computational methods like Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) (e.g., Gibbs sampling, Metropolis-Hastings, Hamiltonian Monte Carlo), samples are drawn from the joint posterior distribution of all model parameters. The result is a set of $M$ posterior samples for each parameter, e.g., $(\alpha^{(m)}, \beta^{(m)}, \gamma^{(m)})$, for $m=1, \dots, M$.
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{Step 2: Prediction/Simulation Step (Posterior Predictive Simulation)}
For each set of sampled parameters from the posterior distribution ($m=1, \dots, M$):
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Generate counterfactual data:
    For each individual $i$ in the observed population (or a large simulated population representative of it), set their treatment to the hypothesized regimen $A^*$.
    Using the $m$-th sample of parameters for the confounder models ($\gamma^{(m)}$), simulate trajectories of time-varying confounders under this regimen. This involves drawing from the conditional predictive distributions of $L_t$ for each individual, sequentially.
    Using the $m$-th sample of parameters for the outcome model ($\alpha^{(m)}, \beta^{(m)}$), predict the outcome for each individual under the simulated covariate and treatment path. This also involves drawing from the conditional predictive distribution of $Y$.

    \item Calculate marginal mean for this sample:
    Average the predicted outcomes across all individuals to obtain the marginal mean outcome for the regimen $A^*$ for this $m$-th posterior sample:
    $$
    \hat{\mathbb{E}}[Y^{A^*}]^{(m)} = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^N \hat{Y}_i(A^*)^{(m)}.
    $$
\end{enumerate}
This results in $M$ samples of the marginal mean outcome $\hat{\mathbb{E}}[Y^{A^*}]^{(m)}$, which together form the posterior distribution of the marginal mean outcome under treatment $A^*$.

\paragraph{Step 3: Causal Effect Estimation}
To estimate the causal effect of two different regimens, $A_1^*$ and $A_0^*$, the above process is repeated to obtain $M$ posterior samples for each: $\hat{\mathbb{E}}[Y^{A_1^*}]^{(m)}$ and $\hat{\mathbb{E}}[Y^{A_0^*}]^{(m)}$.
The posterior distribution of the causal effect is then directly obtained by computing the difference or ratio for each posterior sample:
$$
\Delta^{(m)} = \hat{\mathbb{E}}[Y^{A_1^*}]^{(m)} - \hat{\mathbb{E}}[Y^{A_0^*}]^{(m)} \quad \text{ (for differences)}
$$
$$
\Delta^{(m)} = \frac{\hat{\mathbb{E}}[Y^{A_1^*}]^{(m)}}{\hat{\mathbb{E}}[Y^{A_0^*}]^{(m)}} \quad \text{ (for ratios)}
$$
From this posterior distribution of the causal effect, one can derive point estimates, credible intervals, and posterior probabilities. A key advantages of Bayesian G-computation are that it provides full uncertainty quantification. The posterior distributions for all parameters and causal effects allows for more comprehensive uncertainty assessment. Incorporation of prior knowledge by allowing for the inclusion of expert knowledge or evidence from previous studies through informative prior distributions. Handling complex models since MCMC methods are well-suited for fitting complex hierarchical or non-linear models often encountered in causal inference. Direct probabilistic statements like ``there is a 95\% probability that the causal effect is between X and Y.''


\subsubsection{Multiple Imputation Marginalization method based on parametric G-computation}
Multiple Imputation Marginalization (MIM) aims to recover a compatible marginal treatment effect by integrating or averaging the conditional estimate over the relevant population, particularly useful for generalized linear models or Cox models \citep{RemiroAzocar2022b}.
This is a robust method for estimating marginal causal effects, particularly within the framework of parametric G-computation, while simultaneously addressing missing data in the covariates or outcome. 
Developed by Remiro-Azócar et al., MIM leverages the strengths of multiple imputation \citep{rubin1987} to handle missingness and propagate uncertainty, combined with the power of G-computation to standardize effects across a target population. It is especially useful for estimating marginal effects (e.g., marginal odds ratios) from conditional models, which is crucial for non-collapsible effect measures.

The method integrates two main stages: (1) generation of synthetic datasets (incorporating missing data imputation and counterfactual treatment assignment), and (2) analysis of these synthetic datasets. While it can be implemented with frequentist methods, it naturally accommodates a Bayesian statistical framework, which facilitates principled uncertainty propagation.

\paragraph{Stage 1: Synthetic Data Generation (Imputation and Counterfactual Assignment)}
This stage creates multiple ``complete'' synthetic datasets that represent counterfactual scenarios and account for missing data. It typically involves a Bayesian approach for imputation and predictive modelling.

\begin{enumerate}
    \item Missing Data Imputation Model: Fit a multiple imputation model to the observed (potentially incomplete) dataset. This model imputes missing values for $Y$, $A$, and $L$ (or relevant subsets) based on their observed relationships. In a Bayesian context, this involves sampling from the posterior predictive distribution of the missing data given the observed data and specified imputation models.
    Let $D_{obs}$ be the observed data with potentially missing values. We generate $M$ imputed datasets: $D^{(m)}_{\text{complete}}$, for $m=1, \dots, M$.

    \item Outcome Regression Model (Fitted on Imputed Data): For each of the $M$ imputed datasets, fit a parametric outcome regression model relating the outcome $Y$ to treatment $A$ and covariates $L$.
    For a binary outcome, this might be a logistic regression:
    $$
    P(Y=1 | A=a, L=l; \theta^{(m)}) = g^{-1}(\alpha^{(m)} + \beta_A^{(m)} a + (\beta_L^{(m)})^\top l), 
    $$
    where $g^{-1}(\cdot)$ is the inverse of the link function (e.g., logit inverse). The parameters $(\alpha^{(m)}, \beta_A^{(m)}, \beta_L^{(m)})$ are estimated for each imputed dataset $m$. In a Bayesian setting, these parameters themselves would be drawn from their posterior distribution conditional on $D^{(m)}_{\text{complete}}$.

    \item Counterfactual Data Creation: For each of the $M$ imputed datasets, and for each individual $i$ within that dataset, create two (or more, for multiple regimens) counterfactual copies:
    \begin{itemize}
        \item Set $A=1$: $D_i^{(m, A=1)} = (Y_i^{(m)}, A=1, L_i^{(m)})$
        \item Set $A=0$: $D_i^{(m, A=0)} = (Y_i^{(m)}, A=0, L_i^{(m)})$
    \end{itemize}

    \item Prediction of Counterfactual Outcomes: Using the fitted outcome model from step 2 (with parameters $\theta^{(m)}$) and the counterfactual assignments from step 3, predict the outcome for each individual in each counterfactual dataset.
    For each $m=1, \dots, M$ and for each individual $i$:
    $$ \hat{P}(Y=1 | A=1, L_i^{(m)}; \theta^{(m)}) = g^{-1}(\alpha^{(m)} + \beta_A^{(m)} \cdot 1 + (\beta_L^{(m)})^\top L_i^{(m)}) $$
    $$ \hat{P}(Y=1 | A=0, L_i^{(m)}; \theta^{(m)}) = g^{-1}(\alpha^{(m)} + \beta_A^{(m)} \cdot 0 + (\beta_L^{(m)})^\top L_i^{(m)}) $$
    These predictions are done for each individual within each of the $M$ imputed datasets.
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{Stage 2: Analysis of Synthetic Datasets and Pooling}

\begin{enumerate}
    \item Marginalization within each Imputed Dataset: For each of the $M$ sets of predictions obtained in Stage 1, calculate the marginal expected outcome by averaging the predicted probabilities across all individuals in that imputed dataset.
    For each $m=1, \dots, M$:
    $$
    \hat{\mathbb{E}}[Y^{A=1}]^{(m)} = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^N \hat{P}(Y=1 | A=1, L_i^{(m)}; \theta^{(m)})
    $$
    $$
    \hat{\mathbb{E}}[Y^{A=0}]^{(m)} = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^N \hat{P}(Y=1 | A=0, L_i^{(m)}; \theta^{(m)}).
    $$

    \item Calculate Effect Measures within each Imputed Dataset: From these marginal means, compute the desired effect measure for each $m$. For example, for a marginal odds ratio:
    $$
    \widehat{\text{OR}}^{(m)} = \frac{\hat{\mathbb{E}}[Y^{A=1}]^{(m)} / (1 - \hat{\mathbb{E}}[Y^{A=1}]^{(m)})}{\hat{\mathbb{E}}[Y^{A=0}]^{(m)} / (1 - \hat{\mathbb{E}}[Y^{A=0}]^{(m)})}.
    $$

    \item Pooling Results (Rubin's Rules): The $M$ estimates of the marginal effect measure (e.g., $\widehat{\text{OR}}^{(m)}$) are then combined using Rubin's rules for multiple imputation \citep{rubin1987}. This combines the within-imputation variability with the between-imputation variability to provide a single point estimate and a valid measure of total uncertainty (e.g., standard error or credible interval).

    Let $Q$ be the parameter of interest (e.g., $\widehat{\text{OR}}$).
    The pooled point estimate and total variance are
    \begin{equation}
    \label{eqn:rubins-rules}
    \bar{Q} = \frac{1}{M} \sum_{m=1}^M Q^{(m)} \quad \text{and} \quad
    \text{Var}(\bar{Q}) = \bar{U} + (1 + \frac{1}{M}) B, 
    \end{equation}
    where $\bar{U} = \frac{1}{M} \sum_{m=1}^M U^{(m)}$ is the within-imputation variance (average of squared standard errors from each imputed analysis), and $B = \frac{1}{M-1} \sum_{m=1}^M (Q^{(m)} - \bar{Q})^2$ is the between-imputation variance.
\end{enumerate}

The ``optimization'' in MIM occurs at two levels:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Imputation Model Fitting: The process of generating multiple imputed datasets involves fitting models (often using MCMC in a Bayesian framework, or chained equations) to estimate the missing data conditional on observed data. This is an optimization to characterize the missing data distribution.
    \item Outcome Model Fitting: For each imputed dataset, the parametric outcome regression model parameters are estimated (e.g., via MLE in a frequentist approach, or MCMC in a Bayesian approach). This is the second layer of optimization, finding the best-fitting parameters for the conditional outcome.
\end{enumerate}
The subsequent steps of prediction, marginalization, and pooling are analytical or simulation-based, not further ``optimization'' in the sense of parameter estimation.
Advantages of MIM are that it provides a principled way to incorporate uncertainty due to missing data into the causal effect estimation. MIM also correctly estimates marginal causal effects, overcoming the non-collapsibility issue. It allows for full propagation of uncertainty from missing data, model parameter estimation, and the marginalization process. Finally, it can accommodate various types of outcomes and complex covariate structures.

\subsection{Variance estimation and inference}

\pkg{outstandR} implements three distinct approaches for variance estimation and inference, depending on the chosen strategy and user preference: resampling-based, model-based (Bayesian/Imputation), and robust analytical estimators.

\subsubsection{Resampling and model-based approaches}

By default, the package employs computational methods to estimate uncertainty, reflecting the complexity of population-adjusted estimators where simple analytical variance formulas are often intractable.

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Non-parametric Bootstrapping:} For frequentist strategies such as MAIC and G-computation (ML), standard errors and confidence intervals are derived via non-parametric bootstrapping \citep{efron1979bootstrap}. The IPD is resampled $R$ times, and the entire estimation procedure is repeated for each resample. The variance of the treatment effect is estimated as the sample variance of the $R$ bootstrap replicates.
    
    \item \textbf{Bayesian Posterior Inference:} Inference is fully Bayesian. Uncertainty is quantified using the posterior distribution of the marginal treatment effect, which naturally propagates uncertainty from the outcome model parameters and the missing potential outcomes.
    
    \item \textbf{Rubin's Rules:} For MIM, variance is estimated using Rubin's rules, given in Equation~(\ref{eqn:rubins-rules}) \citep{rubin1987}.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Robust sandwich estimator} \label{sec:variance_methods}
As an alternative to computationally intensive resampling, \pkg{outstandR} also implements a robust sandwich variance estimator for the STC, MAIC, and G-computation (ML) strategies. This provides a computationally efficient way to obtain standard errors that are robust to model misspecification (e.g., heteroscedasticity).

For a marginal treatment effect estimate $\hat{\Delta}$, which is a function $g(\cdot)$ of the estimated model parameters $\hat{\boldsymbol{\beta}}$, the variance is approximated using the delta method:
\begin{equation}
\widehat{\text{Var}}(\hat{\Delta}) = \nabla g(\hat{\boldsymbol{\beta}})^\top \cdot \mathbf{V}_{sand} \cdot \nabla g(\hat{\boldsymbol{\beta}}),
\end{equation}
where $\mathbf{V}_{sand}$ is the heteroscedasticity-consistent (HC) covariance matrix of the regression coefficients $\hat{\boldsymbol{\beta}}$, computed as $\mathbf{H}^{-1} \mathbf{M} \mathbf{H}^{-1}$ where $\mathbf{H}$ is the ``bread'' (Hessian/Fisher Information) and $\mathbf{M}$ is the ``meat'' (outer product of gradients) of the likelihood function. The gradient $\nabla g(\hat{\boldsymbol{\beta}})$ captures how the marginal effect changes with respect to the underlying model parameters and is computed numerically to support complex standardization steps.

The \texttt{outstandR()} function is a high-level wrapper function for carrying out all of the required steps of ITC and population adjustment depending on the chosen method. It is designed to abstract away the task of transporting from the IPD to the ALD population and computing the contrast and absolute statistics of interest. \subsection{The ``strategy'' family of functions}

\subsection{Related R packages}
Several \proglang{R} packages share conceptual ground with the methods implemented here, though they typically differ in scope or intended application.

General-purpose tools for marginal effects and standardization exist, but often focus on single-study contexts. For instance, the \pkg{marginaleffects} package \citep{aari_marginaleffects_2024} offers a comprehensive suite for post-estimation quantities but is not explicitly designed for cross-study population adjustment. Similarly, \pkg{stdReg2} focuses on standardizing outcomes within a single dataset \citep{g_sofer_2023_10022204}.

In the domain of longitudinal data, \pkg{gfoRmula} \citep{sjolander2023gformula} and \pkg{gFormulaMI} \citep{Sterne2023} implement the G-formula to estimate causal effects in the presence of time-varying treatments and confounders. However, these are primarily designed for longitudinal analysis of a single study (the latter incorporating multiple imputation via \pkg{mice}) rather than the synthesis of disparate data sources.

Closer to the context of ITC, the \pkg{multinma} package \citep{phillippo_multinma_2020} implements multilevel network meta-regression to synthesize individual and aggregate data. It addresses population adjustment within a Bayesian framework using numerical integration, distinct from the modular, frequentist G-computation or weighting approaches offered here. Finally, \pkg{maicplus} \citep{maicplus_2024} is a specialist ITC package, but its functionality is strictly limited to the Matching-Adjusted Indirect Comparison (MAIC) methodology.


\subsection{Software architecture and S3 class design}
We adopted a functional object-oriented design using \proglang{R}'s S3 system to balance modularity with ease of use. The core design philosophy separates the \emph{definition} of the statistical approach from its \emph{execution}.
This is achieved through ``strategy'' objects. Functions such as \code{strategy_maic()}, \code{strategy_stc()}, and \code{strategy_gcomp_ml()} do not perform any immediate calculation. Instead, they validate the user's hyperparameters (e.g., formula, error distribution, bootstrap iterations) and return a structured list object with a specific class attribute (e.g., \code{c("maic", "strategy", "list")}).

The main wrapper function \code{outstandR()} remains agnostic to the specific method being used, providing a unified interface. It accepts a \code{strategy} object and delegates the computational heavy lifting to the appropriate backend. This simplifies the user API, as the function signature for \code{outstandR()} remains consistent regardless of whether the user is performing a simple MAIC or a complex Bayesian G-computation. Also, new methods can be added by simply defining a new constructor function (e.g., \code{strategy_newmethod()}) and a corresponding S3 method for the internal generic \code{calc_IPD_stats()}. This allows the package to grow without modifying the core orchestration logic.

The \pkg{outstandR} package utilizes \proglang{R}'s S3 dispatch system to provide a default \texttt{plot()} method. This method automatically extracts both relative contrasts and absolute arm-specific estimates from the results object. Furthermore, the method is designed to accept multiple \texttt{outstandR} objects, enabling direct visual comparison of different adjustment strategies or sensitivity analyses within a single, faceted forest plot.

\subsection{Internal method dispatch}
The \pkg{outstandR} package relies on standard S3 method dispatch to route the analysis to the correct computational engine. The internal generic function \code{calc_IPD_stats()} is the central dispatch point.
When \code{outstandR()} is called, it pre-processes the input data and then calls \code{calc_IPD_stats(strategy, analysis_params)}. \proglang{R}'s S3 system inspects the class of the \code{strategy} object and dispatches to the specific implementation:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \code{calc_IPD_stats.maic()}: Handles propensity score weighting and bootstrap estimation.
  \item \code{calc_IPD_stats.stc()}: Manages outcome regression modelling and prediction.
  \item \code{calc_IPD_stats.gcomp_ml()}: Orchestrates the simulation of pseudo-populations and maximum likelihood estimation.
  \item \code{calc_IPD_stats.gcomp_bayes()}: Interfaces with the \proglang{Stan} backend for Bayesian inference.
\end{itemize}

To adhere to the DRY (Don't Repeat Yourself) principle, we utilized a functional programming pattern with the \code{IPD_stat_factory()} helper. Since many strategies require similar post-processing---such as calculating the Average Treatment Effect (ATE) from arm-specific means and formatting the output list---\code{IPD_stat_factory()} wraps the specific calculation functions. It standardizes the output structure (means, variances, confidence intervals) across all methods, ensuring that the \code{outstandR} return object is consistent and predictable for the user.

\subsection{Data requirements and structure} \label{sec:data-req}
The \pkg{outstandR} package requires two primary inputs: individual patient data (IPD) and aggregate-level data (ALD). While the statistical content varies by outcome type, the structural format is standardized across the package to ensure a consistent workflow.

\subsubsection{Individual patient data (IPD)}
IPD must be provided in long format, where each row represents a single patient. It must contain the following columns:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Outcome (\texttt{y}): A column representing the observed result (e.g., 0/1 for binary, numeric for continuous, or counts).
    \item Treatment (\texttt{trt}): A character or factor column identifying the treatment arm (e.g., ``A'' or ``C'').
    \item Covariates: Separate columns for each prognostic factor (PF) and effect modifier (EM) used in the adjustment.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Aggregate-level data (ALD)}
ALD must be provided in a `tidy' long format to facilitate mapping to IPD covariates. The ALD data frame requires four specific columns:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \texttt{variable}: The name of the covariate or outcome (must match IPD column names).
    \item \texttt{statistic}: The type of summary measure provided (e.g., ``mean'', ``sd'', ``prop'', ``sum'', or ``N'').
    \item \texttt{value}: The numerical value of the statistic.
    \item \texttt{trt}: The treatment label (e.g., ``B'' or ``C''). For baseline covariates assumed to be balanced, this may be \texttt{NA}.
\end{itemize}

The next step is to define the formula, which specifies the relationship between the covariates, treatments and outcome.
For this example, the outcome regression is

$$
\text{logit}(p) = \beta_0 + \beta^{PF}_{1} X^{PF}_{1} + \beta^{PF}_{2 }X^{PF}_{2} + (\beta_{trt} + \beta^{EM}_{1} X^{EM}_{1} + + \beta^{EM}_{2} X^{EM}_{2}) \mathbb{I}(trt = 1),
$$
where $\mathbb{I}()$ is the indicator function, taking value 1 when true and 0 otherwise.
The corresponding \proglang{R} formula used by \pkg{outstandR} is independent of the particular type of data and so we do not include a link function at this stage, i.e, the identity link.
\begin{verbatim}
> # linear formula for the outcome model
> lin_form_contX <- as.formula(
+    "y ~ PF_cont_1 + PF_cont_2 + trt + trt:EM_cont_1 + trt:EM_cont_2")
\end{verbatim}
That is, the main effects are the prognostic factors (\texttt{PF\_cont\_1} and \texttt{PF\_cont\_2}) and the treatment  \texttt{trt}. The interaction terms are with the treatment defined as effect modifiers (\texttt{EM\_cont\_1} and \texttt{EM\_cont\_2}). 

For the MAIC approach, we define the balancing formula as 
\begin{verbatim}
> # linear formula for the outcome model
> bal_form_contX <- as.formula(
+    "~ PF_cont_1 + PF_cont_2 + EM_cont_1 + EM_cont_2")
\end{verbatim}

\paragraph{Relative treatment effects}
Let $p_C$ be the proportion of events in the comparator treatment arm and $p_B$ be the proportion of events in the reference treatment arm. For the logit link with binary data, the mean treatment effect required in Equation~(\ref{eqn:paic}) is the log-odds ratio
$$
\Delta_{BC} = \text{logit}(p_B) - \text{logit}(p_C)
$$
with variance
$$
\text{Var}(\Delta_{BC}) = \frac{1}{y_B} + \frac{1}{N_B - y_B} + \frac{1}{y_C} + \frac{1}{N_C - y_C},
$$
where $y_t$ are the number of events and $N_t$ are the total number of patients for treatment $t$.

A full list including alternative treatment effects and variance components available for binary outcome data is given in Table~\ref{tab:releff}.

\subsection{User-specified outcome scales}
By default, \pkg{outstandR} reports treatment effects on the scale corresponding to the outcome regression's link function. For instance, a logistic regression specified with \texttt{family = binomial(link = "logit")} naturally yields estimates in log-odds ratios, while a linear regression yields mean differences. However, health economic models often require parameters on specific scales, such as risk differences (RD) or relative risks (RR), to facilitate interpretation or align with decision-analytic model inputs.

\subsection{Robust variance estimation}
Standard errors in population-adjusted analyses can be sensitive to model misspecification. Users can request robust (sandwich) standard errors (see Section~\ref{sec:variance_methods}) by setting the \code{var\_method} argument to \code{"sandwich"}. 
% This is particularly useful for methods like STC and G-computation (ML) where the outcome model parameters directly drive the effect estimate.

The following example demonstrates how to compare the naive model-based standard error with the robust sandwich estimate for an STC analysis:

\begin{verbatim}
> # standard G-computation ML analysis (model-based variance)
> stc_naive <- outstandR(
+    ipd_trial = AC_IPD_binY_contX,
+    ald_trial = BC_ALD_binY_contX,
+    strategy = strategy_gcomp_ml(
+      formula = list(outcome_model = lin_form_contX),
+      family = binomial()),
+    seed = 12345,
+    var_method = "sample") # default

> # G-computation ML with robust variance (sandwich estimator)
> stc_robust <- outstandR(
+    ipd_trial = AC_IPD_binY_contX,
+    ald_trial = BC_ALD_binY_contX,
+    strategy = strategy_gcomp_ml(
+      formula = list(oucome_model = lin_form_contX),
+      family = binomial()),
+    seed = 12345,
+    var_method = "sandwich")

> # compare standard errors
> stc_naive$contrasts$var
> stc_robust$contrasts$var

       AB        AC        BC

\end{verbatim}

This option allows analysts to assess the stability of their conclusions without requiring manual implementation of the delta method or bootstrapping.

\subsection{User-defined covariate distributions in G-computation}
By default, the simulation step in G-computation assumes that covariates in the target population follow a multivariate normal distribution, parameterized by the means and standard deviations reported in the ALD (corresponding to $F_k(\cdot; \theta_k)$ in Equation~(\ref{eqn:marginal-transformation})). However, this assumption may be inappropriate. For example, age or costs may be skewed and strictly positive, while binary variables, such as sex, are bounded.

\pkg{outstandR} allows users to specify alternative marginal distributions for specific covariates via the \code{marginal\_distns} argument.

A key feature of this implementation is that the package automatically parameterizes these distributions using the summary statistics available in the ALD. For example, if a user specifies a Gamma distribution for \code{age}, \pkg{outstandR} will automatically retrieve the reported mean ($\mu$) and standard deviation ($\sigma$) from the ALD data frame and convert them into the required shape ($\alpha$) and rate ($\beta$) parameters using the method of moments:
\begin{equation*}
\alpha = (\mu / \sigma)^2, \quad \beta = \mu / \sigma^2
\end{equation*}
This is more robust and streamlines the workflow by removing the need for users to perform these statistical transformations manually.

First, define the target distributions. We specify a Gamma distribution with “gamma” for \texttt{X1} and a Binomial with “binom” for \texttt{X2}. The other covariates not listed (\texttt{X3}, \texttt{X4}) default to a normal distribution, i.e., “norm”.

\begin{verbatim}
> # define distributions
> custom_distns <- c(X1 = "gamma", X2 = "binom")

> # run G-computation ML
> outstandR_custom <- outstandR(
+    ipd_trial = AC_IPD_contY_mixedX,
+    ald_trial = BC_ALD_contY_mixedX,
+    strategy = strategy_gcomp_ml(
+      formula = list(outcome_model = lin_form_mixedX),
+      family = gaussian(link = "identity"),
+      marginal_distns = custom_distns,
+      N = 1000),
+    seed = 12345)

> print(outstandR_custom)

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

The \pkg{outstandR} package offers a comprehensive and flexible toolset for addressing indirect treatment comparisons, especially in the challenging context of limited individual patient data. Its key strengths include:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Versatility: Support for multiple standardisation methods (MAIC, STC, G-computation, MIM) allows users to choose the most appropriate approach for their data and assumptions.
    \item Uncertainty quantification: The Bayesian G-computation approach provides a robust framework for propagating and quantifying uncertainty.
    \item Practical relevance: Directly addresses a critical need in health economics and evidence synthesis for comparing treatments when direct trial data is unavailable.
\end{itemize}
While powerful, \pkg{outstandR} relies on the correct specification of outcome models and the availability of sufficient aggregate-level data for reliable estimation. This is an active area of research. Future work will include expanding the range of supported outcome models and exploring more advanced covariate simulation techniques.
The package’s modular S3 architecture was designed specifically to accommodate such extensions.
